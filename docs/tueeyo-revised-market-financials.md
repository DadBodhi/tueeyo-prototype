## Market Size & Segmentation (Revised)

### A Two-Axis Growth Model

Tueeyo's addressable market expands along two independent axes:

- **Geographic:** Leeds → UK cities → International (Ireland, Spain, US Latin scenes)
- **Dance style:** Latin/Salsa → Partner dance broadly → All class-based dance styles

These axes are independent. Geographic expansion can happen within Latin dance before style expansion begins, and vice versa. This creates natural decision points and multiple paths to growth.

### Market Tiers

| Tier | Community | Est. UK regulars | Tueeyo fit | Launch timing |
|------|-----------|-----------------|------------|---------------|
| **Beachhead** | Latin dance (salsa, bachata, kizomba) | ~18,000 | Perfect — built for this | Year 1–2 |
| **Adjacent** | Other partner dance (tango, ceroc, swing) | ~40,000 | Strong — same platform needs | Year 2–3 |
| **Broader** | All class-based dance styles | 100,000+ | Good — minor adaptation required | Year 4+ |

**Total realistic addressable market (Tiers 1–2): ~55,000–60,000 regular UK class attendees.**  
This is approximately 6x the original estimate, driven primarily by inclusion of the ceroc/Modern Jive community.

### Basis for Estimates

**Latin dance (~18,000):**  
The Leeds salsa Facebook group has 5,200 members with ~200 daily active viewers, suggesting ~400–600 genuinely active Leeds Latin dancers. Leeds is a significant but mid-sized UK scene. Scaled nationally across ~30 cities, 15,000–20,000 regular Latin dancers is a conservative estimate.

**Adjacent partner dance (~40,000):**  
Ceroc/Modern Jive operates 200+ UK venues with typical attendance of 80–150 per venue, implying 20,000–30,000 active participants. Tango, swing, and ballroom communities add a further 15,000–20,000.

**Style overlap (important for modelling):**  
Dancers are not siloed. Within the Latin community there is approximately:
- 70% overlap between salsa, bachata, and kizomba
- 20% overlap into Argentine tango
- 10–15% overlap into ceroc/Modern Jive

This means activating the Leeds salsa scene organically reaches a significant proportion of the Leeds Latin community as a whole, without separate targeting.

### The Community Flywheel

Tueeyo's growth does not follow a smooth adoption curve. It follows a **scene-based flywheel** driven by the highly connected nature of dance communities:

1. A small group of "super node" regulars attend multiple classes per week across multiple schools
2. They check in, making the platform look active to teachers immediately
3. Teachers see check-ins, claim events, upload routine videos
4. Videos gated behind check-in incentivise more students to join
5. More students across multiple schools incentivise teachers to upgrade to Plus tier
6. Plus tier payments/bookings drive further student wallet adoption
7. Wallet use spreads across schools, reinforcing teacher adoption

**Each new city or style scene restarts this flywheel.** Leeds salsa becomes the proof of concept that makes Manchester salsa faster to activate, which makes Leeds bachata faster, and so on.

### Major Events as Acquisition Accelerators

A distinct feature of dance communities is high-attendance regional and national events (e.g. Calle Ocho Leeds: ~200–300 attendees; Blackpool salsa nights: ~700 attendees). These events draw dancers from multiple cities and scenes in a single night.

A single major event can:
- Onboard 50–150 new users from 5–10 different cities
- Activate super nodes in scenes where Tueeyo had no presence
- Drive geographic expansion faster than city-by-city organic growth

Major events occur several times per year and provide recurring acquisition opportunities, not one-off windfalls.

## Financial Projections (Revised — Lean Operations)

### Revenue Model

**Subscription revenue:**
- Plus tier: £20/month per professional
- Pro tier: £50/month per organisation

**Transaction fee revenue:**
- 3.0% at launch (Month 7)
- 3.5% at Month 12
- 4.0% in Year 2
- 4.5% in Year 3
- 5.0% cap from Year 4

**Average student spend assumption:** £24/month (3 × £8 classes)

### Key Observations

**Cash profitable from Year 4.** Under lean operations and revised (higher) user projections driven by the two-axis growth model, the business reaches profitability in Year 4 and generates meaningful lifestyle business income by Year 5.

**Cumulative break-even at Year 4.** Total investment to break-even is approximately £12,000–£15,000 over 3 years — comfortably within bootstrap range.

**Transaction revenue dominates by Year 5 (67% of total).** This is healthy but carries dependency on payment adoption rates. If students are slower to adopt the wallet, subscription revenue remains the floor.

**The hidden cost: founder time.** This model assumes the founder draws no salary for Years 1–3. At an opportunity cost of £30–35k/year, this represents £90–105k of real economic cost not reflected in the table above. This is the true risk of the bootstrap approach and should be weighed accordingly.

**Upside not modelled:**
- International expansion (Ireland, Spain, US Latin scenes)
- Marketplace/affiliate revenue
- White-label licensing to dance organisations
- Non-Latin dance style adoption above base assumptions

These could materially increase Year 4–5 revenue but are deliberately excluded from base projections to maintain credibility.
